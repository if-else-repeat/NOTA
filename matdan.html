Understanding India's Elections

The Four Pillars
of Indian Democracy

India doesn't have one election — it has many, running constantly, at every level of government. Here's the complete picture.

🏛
Every 5 Years
Lok Sabha
The "House of the People." 543 seats, directly elected by citizens through First-Past-The-Post (FPTP) voting. Whoever commands a majority (272+) forms the government. The Prime Minister must maintain this majority to stay in power.
543 Constituencies
🗳
Who Can Vote?
Universal Adult Franchise
Every Indian citizen aged 18+ who is a resident of a polling area and registered on the electoral roll can vote. No property, tax, literacy, or gender requirements. This was revolutionary in 1952 — most countries had restricted suffrage at the time.
Age 18+ Since 1989
📋
The Method
First-Past-The-Post
India uses FPTP — the candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins, even without an absolute majority. Critics argue this can give parties a large number of seats with a smaller share of total votes. The 2024 election saw BJP win 240 seats with ~37% votes.
Winner Takes All
⚡
Technology
EVM + VVPAT
Since 2004, India uses Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs). VVPAT (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail) was added to allow voters to visually confirm their vote. EVMs are standalone, not connected to internet, making remote tampering technically impossible.
Used Since 1999 (Pilot)
🏟
State Legislature
Vidhan Sabha
Each of India's 28 states has its own Vidhan Sabha (legislative assembly). Members are called MLAs (Members of Legislative Assembly). The party with the most seats forms the state government, and its leader becomes the Chief Minister.
28 States · 8 UTs
📅
Timing
Staggered Schedule
State elections happen at different times — roughly every 5 years per state, but they're not synchronized with each other or with the Lok Sabha. This means India is almost always in some state of election, somewhere. This is what the One Nation One Election debate is about.
~5 State Elections/Year
🏢
Bicameral States
Vidhan Parishad
Six states (UP, Bihar, Maharashtra, Karnataka, AP, Telangana) also have a Vidhan Parishad (upper house/legislative council). Members are elected indirectly by MLAs, graduates, teachers, and local body members, or nominated by the Governor.
6 States with Upper House
🎯
President's Rule
Article 356
If no party can form a majority in a state, or if constitutional machinery breaks down, the President can impose direct rule (President's Rule) under Article 356. This dissolves the state assembly and the Centre governs the state, triggering fresh elections.
Used 100+ Times Since 1950
🎩
Upper House
Rajya Sabha
The "Council of States" — 245 members (233 elected + 12 nominated by President for expertise in arts, science, literature). Unlike Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha is a permanent body — it is never dissolved. One-third of its members retire every two years.
6-Year Terms
🗺
Indirect Election
Elected by MLAs
Citizens don't directly vote for Rajya Sabha. Elected members of each state's Vidhan Sabha vote for their state's Rajya Sabha representatives using Single Transferable Vote (STV). Larger states get more seats proportionate to population.
Proportional Representation
⚖
Powers
Role of Rajya Sabha
Most bills must pass both houses. However, Money Bills (budget, taxes) only need Lok Sabha approval. Rajya Sabha can delay but not veto them. Constitutional amendment bills need 2/3 majority in both houses — making Rajya Sabha composition crucial for reforms.
Revising Chamber
🌾
Rural Governance
Panchayati Raj
The 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992) created India's three-tier rural local government: Gram Panchayat (village), Panchayat Samiti (block), and Zila Parishad (district). Over 2.5 million elected representatives, with 1/3 seats reserved for women.
73rd Amendment · 1992
🏙
Urban Governance
Municipal Elections
The 74th Amendment (1992) governs urban local bodies — Municipal Corporations (large cities), Municipal Councils (smaller cities), and Nagar Panchayats (town areas). Mayors and councillors are directly elected. Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru all have large municipal corporations.
74th Amendment · 1992
📊
Scale
The Numbers
India has over 260,000 Gram Panchayats, 6,000+ Panchayat Samitis, 500+ Zila Parishads, and 8,000+ urban local bodies. The sheer scale of local elections dwarfs the Lok Sabha. State Election Commissions (separate from ECI) conduct these elections.
~3M Elected Representatives
♀
Reservation
Women in Local Bodies
One of India's biggest but least-discussed democratic wins: 33% (and in many states, 50%) of seats in local bodies are reserved for women. This has put over 1.3 million women in elected positions at the grassroots — one of the largest in the world.
1.3M+ Elected Women
🎖
Head of State
Presidential Election
India's President is not directly elected by citizens. The electoral college consists of elected MPs (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha) and elected MLAs of all states. Each vote is weighted by the population of the state or the number of MPs. A complex formula ensures fair representation.
Every 5 Years
🏆
Vice President
VP Election
India's Vice President is elected by members of both houses of Parliament (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha) through a secret ballot using Single Transferable Vote. The VP is also the ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha — presiding over all its sessions.
Joint Parliament Vote
⚖
By-Elections
Upachunav
When a sitting MP or MLA dies, resigns, or is disqualified mid-term, a by-election (upachunav) is held for that single seat. These are crucial as they often function as referendums on the ruling government's performance between major elections.
As and When Required
Step by Step

How an Election Actually Works

From the moment the President dissolves Lok Sabha to the day results are declared — here is every step, demystified.

The Election Commission of India (ECI)

Established on January 25, 1950 — a day before India became a republic — the ECI is a constitutional authority that operates completely independently of the government. Its decisions on election schedules, candidate eligibility, and Model Code of Conduct cannot be challenged until after elections. Sukumar Sen was its first Chief Election Commissioner.

Structure
1 Chief Election Commissioner + 2 Election Commissioners. Appointed by President, but can only be removed like a Supreme Court judge — hard to sack, ensuring independence.
State ECs
Separate State Election Commissions handle local body elections (Panchayat & Municipal). They are independent of ECI but similar in structure.
Model Code of Conduct
Once elections are announced, the MCC kicks in — governments can't announce new welfare schemes, make transfers of key officials, or use state machinery for campaigning. A powerful check.
The Scale in 2024
15 million government employees and security personnel deployed. 1 million+ polling booths. No voter more than 2km from their booth. An officer trekked 39km for a single voter in Arunachal Pradesh.
01

Dissolution / Election Announcement

The President dissolves Lok Sabha on the advice of the Council of Ministers (or when term expires). The ECI then announces the election schedule — dates, phases, and the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) immediately takes effect. In 2024, the schedule was announced on March 16 for a 7-phase election.

Triggers MCC
02

Electoral Roll Verification

The ECI maintains electoral rolls — the master list of all eligible voters. Before each election, rolls are updated: new 18-year-olds added, deceased voters removed, addresses corrected. In 2024, over 968 million voters were registered. Citizens check their names on the Voter Service Portal.

Ongoing Process
03

Nomination of Candidates

Candidates file nominations with the Returning Officer of their constituency. Requirements: Indian citizen, 25+ years for Lok Sabha/Vidhan Sabha, 30+ for Rajya Sabha. Must pay a security deposit (₹25,000 for general, ₹12,500 for SC/ST). Deposit is forfeited if they get less than 1/6th of votes.

Form 2A Filing
04

Scrutiny & Withdrawal

After nominations, the Returning Officer examines them for validity. Candidates can withdraw within a specified window. This is when allotment of election symbols happens — parties have reserved symbols; independent candidates pick from unassigned ones. Symbol recognition is crucial for a largely diverse electorate.

Symbol Allotment
05

Campaign Period

Parties and candidates campaign across constituencies. The ECI strictly monitors expenditure limits (₹95 lakh per candidate in larger states for Lok Sabha), prevents use of government resources, cracks down on paid news, and enforces the MCC. NOTA (None of the Above) is also an option on EVMs since 2013.

₹95L Spending Limit
06

Silent Period (48 Hours Before)

48 hours before polling begins in any constituency, all campaigning must stop — no rallies, no canvassing, no exit poll broadcasts allowed. This is the "silent period." Liquor sales are often banned too. The purpose: give voters time to reflect free from campaign noise.

Section 126, RPA 1951
07

Polling Day

Voters visit their assigned booth with Voter ID (EPIC) or 12 other ECI-approved photo IDs. They vote on EVMs; the VVPAT prints a paper slip visible for 7 seconds confirming the vote. In 2024, for the first time, home voting was enabled for voters 85+ and persons with disabilities. Turnout was 642 million — a world record.

EPIC / Aadhaar Accepted
08

Counting & Results

EVMs are sealed and stored under multi-layer security until counting day (often weeks after the last phase). Counting happens simultaneously at constituency counting centers. Results are declared phase by phase, candidates are announced, and the ECI submits the list to the President. Government formation follows.

Under CCTV Surveillance
09

Government Formation

The party/alliance with 272+ seats in Lok Sabha is invited to form the government. The President invites the leader to be Prime Minister. Coalition negotiations happen here. The PM is sworn in, appoints Cabinet ministers, and within 6 months the government must prove majority in Parliament via a confidence vote.

Article 75
Evolution of Democracy

Before Independence, After Independence

India's electoral democracy didn't begin in 1947. Its roots go back to 1909 — and its democratic traditions go back 2,500 years.

1861
Indian Councils Act
The British Parliament, post-1857, created legislative councils in India. But these were small, entirely nominated bodies — no elections whatsoever. Indians had zero say in governance. This was purely advisory to the British Governor.
Fully Nominated
1892
Indian Councils Act — A Crack in the Wall
A limited "indirect election" concept was introduced — certain special constituencies like municipalities and trade associations could recommend (not elect) members to councils. It wasn't democracy, but it was the first crack.
Indirect Recommendations
1909
Morley-Minto Reforms — First Real Elections
The Indian Councils Act 1909 introduced elected (not just recommended) representatives for the first time. However, it also introduced separate electorates for Muslims — a disastrous sowing of communal seeds that contributed to the 1947 partition. The first truly elective element in Indian legislative bodies.
First Elections · Separate Electorates
1919
Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms
The Government of India Act 1919 expanded elected representation and introduced "diarchy" — some provincial subjects transferred to elected Indian ministers. Still deeply limited: only ~10% of the population could vote (based on property and tax qualifications), and critical subjects remained with British-appointed governors.
Dyarchy · 10% Electorate
1935
Government of India Act 1935
The most comprehensive colonial-era constitutional reform. Created a federal structure, expanded provincial autonomy, and significantly widened the electorate to about 35 million voters (up from 7 million). Congress swept the 1937 provincial elections. This act's structure heavily influenced India's 1950 Constitution.
35M Voters · Federal Structure
1946
Constituent Assembly Elections
Elections were held for the Constituent Assembly — the body that would write India's Constitution. Only ~1,500 members of provincial assemblies voted. The Congress dominated. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, despite losing his own seat from Bombay (he was defeated), was elected from Bengal and went on to chair the Drafting Committee.
Constitution Begins
1950
ECI Established & Universal Suffrage Born
January 25, 1950: The Election Commission of India is established. January 26: India becomes a republic. For the first time in history, ALL adult citizens — regardless of gender, caste, religion, education, or wealth — get equal voting rights. 173 million people become eligible voters overnight. The world had never seen anything like it.
173M Voters · First Ever Universal Suffrage at Scale
1951–52
The Great Election — First Lok Sabha
68 phases. 196,084 polling booths. 45.7% turnout. 85% of voters were illiterate — so each candidate got a different coloured ballot box with their symbol. 1,874 candidates across 53 parties. Congress won 364 of 489 seats. Nehru became PM. Master Shyam Saran Negi of Himachal Pradesh cast India's first vote. The world watched in awe.
World's Largest Election (at the time)
1967
Last Simultaneous Elections (Until Now)
For the first four elections (1952, 1957, 1962, 1967), Lok Sabha and all state assemblies voted simultaneously. 1967 was the last time. In 1968-69, several state governments fell prematurely due to party defections, breaking the synchronized cycle — forever changing India's electoral calendar.
Sync Broken · Never Restored
1975–77
The Emergency — Democracy's Darkest Hour
PM Indira Gandhi declared a National Emergency on June 25, 1975, citing internal disturbance. Civil liberties were suspended, opposition leaders jailed, press censored, elections postponed. When finally held in 1977, Indians delivered their verdict decisively: Congress was routed, Janata Party won, and Morarji Desai became India's first non-Congress PM. Democracy proved resilient.
First Electoral Rejection of Congress
1989
Voting Age Lowered to 18
The 61st Constitutional Amendment lowered the voting age from 21 to 18, adding approximately 50 million young voters to the electoral rolls overnight. India recognized that young citizens had a stake in governance long before they turned 21. Today, voters 18-25 are one of the largest and most contested demographic blocs.
+50M New Voters
1992
73rd & 74th Amendments — Grassroots Revolution
PM Narasimha Rao's government passed landmark amendments that constitutionalized local self-government. Panchayati Raj (rural) and Municipal Corporations (urban) were given constitutional status. One-third of seats reserved for women. This created the world's largest experiment in grassroots democracy — 3+ million elected representatives.
Panchayati Raj · 3M Representatives
2013
NOTA — None of the Above
Following a Supreme Court order, NOTA (None of the Above) was introduced on EVMs. Voters who find no candidate worthy can now express their rejection formally. While NOTA cannot cancel an election, it creates political pressure and gives citizens a way to register dissent without abstaining entirely.
Voter Rejection Option
1982
First EVM Pilot
India first tested Electronic Voting Machines in 50 booths in Kerala's Parur constituency in 1982. The EVMs were manufactured by Electronics Corporation of India (ECIL) and Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL). There was massive political resistance — some parties feared manipulation. Legal challenges delayed full rollout for nearly two decades.
Kerala · First 50 Booths
1999–2004
Full EVM Rollout
EVMs were used alongside ballot papers in 1999, and exclusively across all constituencies in the 2004 general election. This eliminated invalid votes (previously up to 8% of all votes), speeded up counting from days to hours, and dramatically reduced booth capturing — you can't stuff ballot boxes you can't open.
Zero Invalid Votes
2013
VVPAT Introduction
Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail added as a paper receipt system. A voter sees a paper slip of whom they voted for for 7 seconds (then it drops into a sealed box) — providing physical verification. After the 2019 election, the Supreme Court ruled that VVPAT slips from 5 randomly selected booths per constituency must be counted.
Physical Verification
2015
Voter Helpline 1950 & Digital Services
ECI launched the Voter Helpline (1800-111-950), Voter Portal, and later the NVSP (National Voter's Service Portal) and Voter Helpline App. Citizens can now register, check their names, find their booth, request vote transfers, and report violations — all digitally.
Digital Democracy
2024
Home Voting & AI-Monitored Booths
For the first time, ECI introduced home voting for citizens 85+ and persons with disabilities. AI-powered cameras and surveillance at sensitive booths. Social media monitoring cells to detect election code violations. 642 million voters participated — the largest single election in human history.
World Record · 642M Votes Cast
The Forgotten Legacy

Democracy Didn't Begin
in Athens.

While the Western world claims Athenian democracy (508 BCE) as the origin of democratic governance, archaeological and textual evidence points to something older — the Gana Sanghas (clan republics) of ancient India, particularly the Licchavi republic of Vaishali in present-day Bihar.

"Republican forms of government existed in many parts of ancient India. There are numerous references to such governments in Buddhist literature. Even in the 4th century BC, there was a republican federation known as the Kshudrak-Malla Sangha, which offered strong resistance to Alexander the Great."

— Election Commission of India Report, First General Elections 1951–52

The Licchavi Gana Sangha had a governing assembly of 7,707 elected members — called Rajas — who deliberated and voted on matters of governance. They elected a President (Raja), Vice-President (Upa-Raja), Defence Chief (Senapati), and Treasurer (Bhandargarika).

The Buddhist Mahavagga text records detailed parliamentary procedures for the Sangha — quorum requirements, voting by ballot (Salaka), a presiding officer (Vinayadhara), and even a political whip (Ganapuraka) ensuring proper voting procedure. These are strikingly similar to modern parliamentary rules.

~600 BCE 7,707 Assembly Members Ballot Voting System Elected Executive Vajji Confederacy

Key Principles of Ancient Gana Republics

Salaka Voting — Ballot System

+
Assembly members voted using Salaka — physical ballots or tokens. A designated Salaka-Grahapaka (vote collector) impartially collected these votes. The process mirrors a secret ballot. This is documented in the Mahavagga — one of the oldest texts describing formalized voting procedures in human history.

Ganapuraka — The Political Whip

+
The Gana Sanghas had a Ganapuraka — an officer appointed specifically to ensure proper voting procedures were followed. This is essentially an ancient equivalent of a parliamentary whip. The existence of such a role suggests these assemblies were sophisticated enough to need enforcement of procedural rules.

Buddhist Sanghas as Democratic Models

+
The Buddha structured his Sangha (monastic community) explicitly on the model of the Licchavi republic. The Buddhist Sangha had formal rules for quorum, debate, voting, and appeals. Historian R.C. Majumdar noted in 1918 that Buddhist procedures reflect "a sophisticated and widespread political culture based on the popular assembly" — predating Greek democracy.

Limitations — Who Could Vote?

+
These weren't universal democracies by modern standards. Voting rights were restricted to male Kshatriyas of the founding clans — the Rajakulas. Women, lower castes (Dasa-Karmakara), and non-citizens couldn't vote. It was oligarchic republicanism, not mass democracy. This distinction is important: ancient India pioneered representative governance, but it was not the equal suffrage we practice today.

Why Did the Republics Fall?

+
The Gana Sanghas were absorbed by the expanding Magadha Empire under Ajatashatru (5th century BCE) and later by Chandragupta Maurya. Ironically, the Arthashastra — Chanakya's treatise on statecraft written for Chandragupta — discusses how kings can infiltrate and destroy republican clans through internal discord. The very advisor who helped India's first empire was a manual on dismantling republics.

Vaishali vs. Athens: Vaishali's republican system likely emerged around 600 BCE — nearly a century before Cleisthenes's democratic reforms in Athens (508 BCE). India's ancient republican tradition predates the Western democratic narrative — a fact rarely taught in schools.

State by State

28 States, 8 Union Territories

Each state is an electoral universe of its own — with its own dominant parties, political culture, number of Lok Sabha seats, and election history.

Uttar Pradesh
80 LS Seats · 403 MLA Seats
India's most populous state (230M). The king-maker in every Lok Sabha election. Whoever wins UP often wins India. Fiercely contested with BJP, SP, BSP, and Congress all historically significant.
Bihar
40 LS Seats · 243 MLA Seats
Historically significant as Bihar was home to Vaishali — the world's first republic. Today dominated by coalition politics with JDU, RJD, BJP, and Congress all playing key roles.
Madhya Pradesh
29 LS Seats · 230 MLA Seats
India's geographical heart. Traditionally a BJP stronghold but has seen tight Congress competition. A large tribal population makes scheduled area seats significant here.
Rajasthan
25 LS Seats · 200 MLA Seats
Famous for its "anti-incumbency" voting pattern — for nearly 30 years, the state has alternated between BJP and Congress every single election cycle.
Delhi
7 LS Seats · 70 MLA Seats
A Union Territory but treated like a state due to its elected government (National Capital Territory). AAP's surprise 2015 sweep (67/70 seats) became one of the most stunning electoral victories in Indian history.
Haryana
10 LS Seats · 90 MLA Seats
A highly competitive state where rural-urban divide, Jat vs. non-Jat politics, and farmers' issues heavily influence outcomes. Elections here are often nail-biters.
Punjab
13 LS Seats · 117 MLA Seats
Historically a Congress-Akali Dal battle, disrupted dramatically when AAP won 92 of 117 seats in 2022 — the party's first state government. Farmers' issues dominate electoral politics.
Himachal Pradesh
4 LS Seats · 68 MLA Seats
A small hill state that historically alternates between BJP and Congress with rare exceptions. Known for extremely high voter participation, often above 75%.
Tamil Nadu
39 LS Seats · 234 MLA Seats
A state where Dravidian parties (DMK, AIADMK) have dominated since 1967. National parties (BJP, Congress) are largely dependent on alliances with these powerful regional players here.
Andhra Pradesh
25 LS Seats · 175 MLA Seats
Bifurcated in 2014 when Telangana was carved out. Strong regional party tradition with TDP and YSRCP alternating. In 2024, AP and Lok Sabha elections happened simultaneously.
Telangana
17 LS Seats · 119 MLA Seats
India's newest state (2014), formed from Andhra Pradesh. Was dominated by BRS (formerly TRS) until Congress's 2023 surprise victory. A state still finding its electoral identity.
Karnataka
28 LS Seats · 224 MLA Seats
A three-way contest between BJP, Congress, and JD(S). Anti-incumbency is strong. The state that produced some of India's most dramatic election reversals — including BJP's loss of 135 seats in 2023.
Kerala
20 LS Seats · 140 MLA Seats
India's most literate state (96%). The only state that has never elected BJP to power. LDF (Left) and UDF (Congress-led) have alternated in government for 50+ years with near-clockwork precision.
Goa
2 LS Seats · 40 MLA Seats
India's smallest state. Politics here is hyperlocal — individual personalities and "switchers" (defecting MLAs) often determine government formation. Notoriously fluid party loyalties.
West Bengal
42 LS Seats · 294 MLA Seats
One of India's most politically significant and competitive states. TMC vs BJP rivalry is intense, often violent. West Bengal's 42 Lok Sabha seats make it crucial — and Mamata Banerjee a major national force.
Odisha
21 LS Seats · 147 MLA Seats
Dominated by BJD (Biju Janata Dal) under Naveen Patnaik for 24 consecutive years (2000–2024) — one of India's longest continuous state governments. BJP broke that streak in 2024.
Jharkhand
14 LS Seats · 81 MLA Seats
Carved from Bihar in 2000, representing tribal areas. Politics revolves around tribal identity, mining rights, and economic development. JMM and BJP are the main contenders.
Chhattisgarh
11 LS Seats · 90 MLA Seats
Formed from MP in 2000. Has large tribal and Naxal-affected regions which create unique electoral challenges. BJP and Congress alternate in power. Security forces play an unusual role in facilitating elections here.
Maharashtra
48 LS Seats · 288 MLA Seats
India's richest state and home to Mumbai. 48 Lok Sabha seats make it the second most important state after UP. Complex coalition politics — the Shiv Sena split and NCP split in 2022-23 created one of India's most dramatic political sagas.
Gujarat
26 LS Seats · 182 MLA Seats
PM Modi's home state, BJP stronghold since 1995. Known for "Gujarat Model" of development-centric politics. Congress has been nearly wiped out here. Extremely high development metrics relative to state average.
Uttarakhand
5 LS Seats · 70 MLA Seats
Created in 2000 from UP. BJP stronghold. Known for high voter turnout among hill communities. Often called a "swing" state in state elections but consistently BJP in national elections.
Assam
14 LS Seats · 126 MLA Seats
The gateway to the Northeast. Issues of illegal immigration (NRC), Citizenship Amendment Act, and tribal rights dominate elections. BJP made major inroads here after decades of Congress dominance.
Manipur
2 LS Seats · 60 MLA Seats
A state with intense ethnic complexity — Meitei, Kuki, and Naga communities with separate geographic and political identities. The 2023 ethnic violence raised deep questions about electoral representation of minorities.
Nagaland
1 LS Seat · 60 MLA Seats
Unique state where elections are often contested by local tribal councils. The Nagaland Tribes Council has historically called for election boycotts linked to the Naga political issue. National parties depend heavily on alliances.
Meghalaya
2 LS Seats · 60 MLA Seats
A matrilineal society where women hold considerable social power, yet political representation remains low. Hung assemblies are common — coalition building is the norm, not the exception.
Arunachal Pradesh
2 LS Seats · 60 MLA Seats
India's easternmost state, bordering China. BJP has made it a stronghold. Famous for the extraordinary logistical challenge of elections — polling officers trek for days through remote terrain to reach isolated voters.
Mizoram · Sikkim · Tripura
1 LS Seat each
Three smaller northeastern states with distinct political identities. Mizoram's MNF and ZPM, Sikkim's SKM, and Tripura's Left-BJP battle — all unique political ecosystems with high voter participation rates.
Jammu & Kashmir
6 LS Seats · 90 MLA Seats
Reorganized as a Union Territory in 2019 after Article 370 abrogation. Assembly elections were held in 2024 — the first in 10 years — with NC winning. A deeply significant and emotionally charged electorate.
Ladakh
1 LS Seat · No Assembly
Carved from J&K in 2019. Currently governed directly by LG. Demands for statehood and a legislature have grown since 2019. No assembly elections held; only Lok Sabha elections.
Puducherry
1 LS Seat · 30 MLA Seats
A UT with legislature. French colonial heritage visible in architecture and law. Congress and AINRC are the main parties. One of India's smallest electorates — every vote here is extremely competitive.
Chandigarh · Lakshadweep · Andaman & Nicobar · Dadra & DNH · Daman & Diu
1 LS Seat each (or shared)
These UTs are governed directly by the Centre via LGs. They elect MPs but have no state assemblies. Lakshadweep and Andaman elections involve extraordinary logistical challenges due to island terrain.
Where We Stumbled

India's Electoral Lessons

Democracy is not just about its triumphs — it grows through its failures. These are the moments India's democracy faltered, and what it learned.

1975–1977
The Emergency
Prime Minister Indira Gandhi invoked Article 352 to declare a national emergency, suspended civil liberties, imprisoned opposition leaders including JP Narayan, Jay Prakash Narayan, Atal Bihari Vajpayee, and L.K. Advani, censored the press, and postponed elections. Democracy was put on hold for 21 months.
India passed the 44th Amendment (1978) to make Emergency harder to impose. Internal Disturbance no longer sufficient — requires "armed rebellion." The judiciary's independence was also strengthened after Emergency-era subservience.
1967–1985
The Aaya Ram, Gaya Ram Era
After 1967's defection crisis, political defections became epidemic — MLAs would switch parties mid-term for money or ministerships. In 1967, Haryana MLA Gaya Lal changed parties three times in a single day, coining the infamous phrase "Aaya Ram, Gaya Ram." Governments were toppled purely through purchased loyalty.
The 52nd Amendment (1985) introduced the Anti-Defection Law — an MLA/MP who votes against their party's line or abstains can be disqualified by the Speaker. While imperfect, it reduced casual floor-crossing. Still debated as potentially anti-democratic by some legal scholars.
1968–1970
Breakdown of Simultaneous Elections
The cycle of simultaneous state and national elections, which had worked smoothly from 1952 to 1967, broke down when multiple state assemblies were dissolved prematurely due to defections and coalition collapses. In 1970, even Lok Sabha was dissolved early. India has been living with a fractured election calendar ever since — essentially always in campaign mode somewhere.
The current One Nation One Election proposal is a direct attempt to fix this 55-year-old problem. However, critics argue this requires fundamental changes to how parliamentary democracy works, and could harm federalism if states' tenures are truncated to align with Lok Sabha.
1984 & Others
Booth Capturing & Electoral Violence
Booth capturing — where armed goons stuff ballot boxes or intimidate voters — was rampant in states like UP, Bihar, and West Bengal from the 1970s through 1990s. Bihar's elections were particularly notorious. Electoral violence claimed hundreds of lives. This was a fundamental assault on democratic integrity.
T.N. Seshan's tenure as CEC (1990–96) was transformative — he enforced the MCC ruthlessly, used the ECI's constitutional independence to repoll entire constituencies, and deployed security forces to prevent violence. His successors continued this legacy. EVMs also made booth capturing meaningless from the 2000s.
Ongoing
Money & Criminalization in Politics
Despite spending limits, the actual money spent in Indian elections is vastly higher. The 2024 Lok Sabha election was estimated to cost ₹1.35 lakh crore (~$16 billion) — making it possibly the world's most expensive election. 46% of candidates in the 2024 Lok Sabha had declared criminal cases. The nexus between money, muscle, and politics remains one of India's deepest democratic challenges.
ECI requires mandatory disclosure of assets and criminal records. Voters can access this on the KYC (Know Your Candidate) portal. Supreme Court has repeatedly asked Parliament to disqualify candidates with convictions. But law reform has been slow — politicians are understandably reluctant to legislate against themselves.
Colonial Legacy
Separate Electorates — The Original Sin
The 1909 Morley-Minto Reforms introduced separate electorates for Muslims — meaning only Muslims could vote for Muslim seats. This politically institutionalized religious identity in voting, and planted seeds of division that contributed to the demand for Pakistan and ultimately the partition of 1947, which killed up to 2 million people.
India's Constitution explicitly rejected separate electorates — all citizens vote in the same constituency regardless of religion. However, reservations for SCs and STs (not separate electorates, but reserved seats) were retained as a corrective for historical exclusion. The distinction matters: reserved seats ensure representation, not segregation.
The Ongoing Debate

One Nation,
One Election

The most debated electoral reform in India right now. Bill introduced in Parliament in December 2024, currently under JPC review. Here's the full picture — without the political noise.

What is it? The proposal to synchronize all elections — Lok Sabha, all 28 state assemblies, and eventually local body elections — so they all happen in the same window (possibly the same day in phases). India did this from 1952 to 1967. The Constitution (129th Amendment) Bill, 2024 was introduced on December 17, 2024 and referred to a 39-member Joint Parliamentary Committee. If ONOE gets implemented, the earliest possible date is 2034.

Arguments For

✓Reduces Cost: India spends an estimated ₹4,000+ crore on each general election. Running elections every few months multiplied across states means India is always spending on elections. ONOE could dramatically cut cumulative costs.
✓Reduces MCC Paralysis: The Model Code of Conduct freezes government policy announcements during elections. With staggered elections, some states are almost always under MCC, paralyzing development schemes for months per year.
✓Governance Focus: Politicians and parties can focus on governance rather than perpetual campaign mode. The argument: governments should govern, not constantly campaign.
✓Historical Precedent: India successfully ran simultaneous elections from 1952 to 1967. It's not an untested experiment — it's a restoration of the original design.
✓Global Examples: Sweden (national + regional + local simultaneously since 1971), Belgium, and Nepal all run simultaneous elections successfully.
✓Public Support: The Kovind Committee received 21,500+ responses — 80% in favour of simultaneous elections.

Arguments Against

✗Federalism Risk: State assembly terms may be truncated to align with Lok Sabha. Critics argue this overrides states' constitutional rights and centralizes power — potentially violating the basic structure of federalism.
✗National Issues Dominate Local: When national and state elections coincide, voters tend to vote on national themes — suppressing state-specific or local issues. Regional parties and local concerns lose salience.
✗What if Government Falls? If a state government loses its majority mid-term, do we hold by-elections or impose President's Rule until the next synchronized window? Both options are problematic.
✗Constitutional Complexity: Requires amending multiple constitutional articles (82, 83, 172, 327), and crucially — some amendments need ratification by at least 50% of states. Opposition states may refuse.
✗Massive Logistics: Conducting all elections simultaneously requires doubling the number of EVMs (currently ~40 lakh), security personnel, and election machinery. The ECI has flagged these concerns.
✗Who Decides "Appointed Date"? The President (acting on Centre's advice) decides when to notify ONOE. Opposition parties argue this gives the ruling party at Centre enormous power to choose election timing advantageously.

Current Status (May 2026): The 129th Constitutional Amendment Bill is under review by the 39-member Joint Parliamentary Committee chaired by BJP MP P.P. Chaudhary. The Bill does NOT include local body elections (Municipal/Panchayat). Earliest implementation: 2034, assuming the 18th and 19th Lok Sabhas both complete full terms.

What Comes Next

The Future of
Indian Democracy

India's electoral system is constantly evolving. Here are the key developments, debates, and directions shaping the next decade of Indian democracy.

01
Women's Reservation Act (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam)
The 106th Constitutional Amendment (2023) reserves 33% of Lok Sabha and state assembly seats for women. But there's a catch: it only comes into effect after the next delimitation exercise, which itself can't happen until after the first census post-2026. The earliest this can be implemented is around 2029–34.
02
Delimitation Post-2026 Census
India's constituency boundaries have been frozen since 1976 (based on 1971 census). The 84th Amendment froze them till 2026. After the upcoming census, constituencies will be redrawn based on new population data. Southern states, which have better population control, fear losing seats to northern states — a major political flashpoint.
03
Electoral Bond System — Post-Supreme Court
The Supreme Court struck down India's Electoral Bond scheme in February 2024, calling it unconstitutional for enabling anonymous political funding. The question now: what replaces it? How India designs its political financing system will significantly shape the money-politics nexus. A major unresolved issue entering the next elections.
04
Remote Voting for Migrant Workers
ECI has proposed Remote Electronic Voting Machines (R-EVMs) to allow internal migrants to vote in their home constituencies from where they work. A prototype was demonstrated in 2023. If implemented, this could enfranchise 300+ million internal migrants who currently cannot practically exercise their vote.
05
AI & Deepfake Regulation in Elections
The 2024 election saw the first widespread use of AI-generated content, voice clones of politicians, and deepfake videos. ECI issued guidelines but enforcement lagged behind technology. Future elections will need robust legal frameworks to combat AI disinformation — a global challenge India must solve at an unprecedented scale of 1 billion+ voters.
06
Ranked Choice or Proportional Representation?
India's First-Past-The-Post system means parties can win massive majorities with minority vote shares. In 2024, BJP won 240 seats with ~37% of votes; Congress won 99 seats with ~21% of votes. Constitutional experts and electoral reformers debate whether India should move to Proportional Representation or at minimum rank-choice voting for better representation of voter preferences.
